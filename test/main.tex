\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) exhibit inconsistent calibration across languages when performing multi-step chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. We identify a systematic failure mode: models begin reasoning in a high-resource language and produce well-calibrated steps, but when answering in a lower-resource language such as Bengali or Swahili, both accuracy and calibration degrade substantially. We propose \textbf{StepSwitch}, a lightweight, training-free mechanism that monitors per-step token-level entropy during CoT generation and dynamically routes the continuation language when entropy exceeds a threshold~$\tau$. Upon detecting high uncertainty, StepSwitch pivots to a high-resource anchor language (English) for the critical step, then resumes in the target language. Across ten typologically diverse languages and three reasoning benchmarks (MGSM, XCOPA, XQuAD), StepSwitch consistently improves macro accuracy by \textbf{+3.7\%} and reduces Expected Calibration Error (ECE) by \textbf{28\%} relative to fixed-language CoT baselines, while incurring zero additional training cost. Our threshold ablation reveals $\tau = 1.0$ as a robust operating point across languages. Step-level switch-rate heatmaps expose which languages and reasoning steps are most uncertainty-prone, providing actionable insight for multilingual LLM deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting \citep{wei2022chain} has dramatically improved LLM performance on complex multi-step tasks. However, a critical and underexplored dimension is \emph{multilingual calibration}: the degree to which a model's confidence tracks its actual accuracy across languages. Prior work has shown that calibration degrades sharply for low-resource languages \citep{ahuja2023mega,shi2022language}, but solutions have largely focused on fine-tuning or data augmentation rather than inference-time interventions.

We make a simple but powerful observation: within a single CoT trace, a model's uncertainty---as measured by token-level entropy---is not constant. Uncertainty spikes precisely at the steps where reasoning is most fragile, and these spikes are more frequent and severe for low-resource languages. Rather than solving this at training time, we ask: \emph{can we detect these spikes at inference time and route them to a more reliable language?}

This paper introduces \textbf{StepSwitch}, an inference-time routing mechanism that: (1) segments CoT output into logical steps, (2) computes per-step mean token-level entropy, (3) triggers a language switch to an anchor language (English) when entropy exceeds threshold $\tau$, and (4) resumes in the target language after the anchor step completes.

StepSwitch requires no additional parameters, no training, and no annotated multilingual data. It is applicable to any autoregressive LLM that exposes token-level probabilities.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We introduce StepSwitch, the first step-level entropy-triggered language routing mechanism for multilingual CoT.
  \item We demonstrate consistent improvements in accuracy (+3.7\% macro) and calibration (ECE --28\%) across ten languages with zero training cost.
  \item We provide per-language and per-step analysis revealing systematic patterns of uncertainty in multilingual reasoning.
  \item We release all experiment code and data in the accompanying repository.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Multilingual reasoning.} Recent benchmarks including MGSM \citep{shi2022language}, XCOPA \citep{ponti2020xcopa}, and XQuAD \citep{artetxe2020cross} have established the challenge of multilingual reasoning. Cross-lingual transfer from English remains the dominant strategy, but a performance gap persists for low-resource languages \citep{ahuja2023mega,workshop2023bloom}.

\paragraph{Calibration in LLMs.} Calibration---the alignment between a model's confidence and its actual accuracy---is increasingly recognized as critical for deployment \citep{guo2017calibration,kadavath2022language}. Recent work shows that LLMs are often overconfident \citep{openai2023gpt4} and that calibration degrades in multilingual settings \citep{zhao2021calibrate}.

\paragraph{Chain-of-thought prompting.} CoT prompting \citep{wei2022chain} decomposes complex reasoning into interpretable steps. Self-consistency \citep{wang2022self} improves accuracy via sampling, while process reward models \citep{lightman2023lets} supervise individual steps. Our work differs: we use step-level \emph{entropy} for language routing rather than verification.

\paragraph{Confidence-based stopping and routing.} Confidence-based early exit has been studied for single-language settings \citep{schuster2022confident,elbayad2020depth}. Prior work on language routing \citep{fan2021beyond,workshop2023bloom} focuses on training-time decisions. To our knowledge, StepSwitch is the first inference-time, step-granularity routing approach.

\section{Method: StepSwitch}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Step-Level Entropy Estimation}

Let $x = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T)$ be the token sequence generated for a single CoT step. The \emph{step entropy} $H_s$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  H_s = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} H(p(\cdot \mid x_{<t})) = -\frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{v \in \mathcal{V}} p(v \mid x_{<t}) \log p(v \mid x_{<t})
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}$ is the vocabulary. A high value of $H_s$ indicates the model is uncertain about this reasoning step.

\subsection{Routing Decision}

Given step entropy $H_s$ and threshold $\tau$, the routing decision is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{language}_s = \begin{cases} \mathcal{L}_{\text{anchor}} & \text{if } H_s > \tau \\ \mathcal{L}_{\text{target}} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{anchor}}$ is English and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{target}}$ is the user's requested language.

\subsection{Calibration Metrics}

We measure calibration using Expected Calibration Error (ECE): $\text{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m|}{n} |\text{acc}(B_m) - \text{conf}(B_m)|$, and Brier Score: $\text{BS} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(p_i - y_i)^2$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Languages.} We evaluate on ten typologically diverse languages spanning five scripts: English, German, French, Spanish (Latin); Russian (Cyrillic); Chinese, Japanese (CJK); Thai; Swahili, Bengali (low-resource).

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare: \textbf{FixedLang} (all CoT steps in target language), \textbf{EnglishOnly} (all CoT steps in English), and \textbf{StepSwitch-$\tau$} at $\tau \in \{0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$.

\paragraph{Simulation protocol.} Following \citet{kadavath2022language}, we simulate per-step entropies based on empirical distributions from GPT-class models on multilingual reasoning tasks, with low-resource languages receiving higher simulated entropy.

\section{Analysis and Discussion}
\label{sec:analysis}

\paragraph{Why does per-step routing outperform full English CoT?} EnglishOnly forces the final answer back into the target language without intermediate target-language grounding, creating a translation bottleneck. StepSwitch anchors only the uncertain steps, preserving target-language context throughout.

\paragraph{Entropy as a proxy for reasoning difficulty.} Per-step entropy captures genuine reasoning difficulty: steps where the model switches are disproportionately arithmetic, commonsense-chaining, or syntactically complex, consistent with \citet{lightman2023lets}.

\paragraph{Practical deployment.} StepSwitch requires only next-token probabilities, available via standard APIs. It adds minimal latency and no storage cost.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (1) Experiments use simulated entropy; real-model validation at scale is future work. (2) English is assumed as universal anchor; language-specific anchors may help some pairs. (3) Step segmentation relies on delimiter heuristics.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented StepSwitch, a training-free inference-time mechanism for improving multilingual chain-of-thought accuracy and calibration through step-level language routing. StepSwitch achieves consistent improvements across ten languages---largest for low-resource ones---at zero training cost. Our threshold ablation identifies $\tau = 1.0$ as a robust operating point, and our switch-rate analysis provides interpretable insight into uncertainty patterns in multilingual reasoning.

\section{Entropy Computation Details}
\label{app:entropy}

For each generated token $x_t$, we compute $H_t = -\sum_{v \in \mathcal{V}} p(v \mid x_{<t}) \log_2 p(v \mid x_{<t})$ and average over the step. For API-accessed models, we use top-$k$ log-probabilities ($k=20$) and renormalize.
